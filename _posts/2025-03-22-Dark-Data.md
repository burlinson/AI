Companies have mountains of data outside of their core databases. If AI could access that ‘dark data’ it might well transform how a business operates and makes decisions. Almost every firm has decades of unstructured content that remains untouched, unclassified, and unanalyzed. The list is endless: emails, logs, dashboards, calendars, meeting notes, call transcripts, images, PDFs, spreadsheets, IM chats, and text messages. 

And that's just scratching the surface. 

For years these mountains of dark data have just not been worth the ROI to embed into a company’s data ecosystem. 

AI fundamentally changes that value equation. 

In the age of AI, dark data can be processed, analyzed and connected at speed, providing unique insights, leading to a competitive advantage.

AI systems are only as strong as the data they can learn from. When organizations rely solely on their legacy structured and curated databases, they are limiting the intelligence and contextual depth of their AI models. 

Dark data contains the institutional memory of the enterprise. These are the nuances, exceptions and tribal knowledge that structured systems simply cannot capture. 

![Iceberg]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/img/Iceberg.webp){: .mx-auto.d-block :}

Unlocking a corporation's dark data allows AI to move beyond generic responses that apply to all companies, and instead deliver unique insights into how a business really works in the real world. That includes how suppliers and clients behave also. 

AI hallucinations occur when models lack grounding in the real world. By indexing and connecting dark data through vector databases and retrieval pipelines, organizations give AI access to real and meaningful knowledge. 

Microsoft is obviously in pole position to help firm's access ther dark data. But this just highlights the governance minefield around dark data. 

Let's imagine Microsoft did suddenly start feeding a decade of dark data into a firm's AI model. All those IMs that employees have assumed are private (they aren't) are suddenly embedded into the model. It knows which managers are liked and what employees really think about their firm's leadership. 

While that might not be a bad thing, it also raises all kinds of legal and ethical questions. Not to mention, employees will start using chat very differently. (Heads up: You migh want to start doing that today, because the question is not **when** firms start feeding dark data to their AI models, it's **when** this will happen!)

The point here is detailed and well thought through governance needs to be in place before companies start loading dark data into their AI models. That's not quick and will need ongoing maintenance. (New job **created** by AI, anyone?)

However, the effort to create and maintain that governance model still provides firms with a positive ROI on embedding dark data into their proprietary AI models.

Companies that embed dark data into their AI models will deliver substantially better customer experiences and accelerate decision making by many multiples. 

Firms that ignore their dark data are falling into the trap of not appreciating that AI has busted legacy ROI models wide open. 

In its simplest form, the promise of AI is the ability to have AI perform the equivalent work of entire teams of employees, but do it twenty-four-seven in a fraction of the time. 

That baseline promise is going to bust multiple business processes and models that have been designed on the premise that effort and output cannot exceed the number of employees.
